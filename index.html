A Journey Through Time

The Eras of Myanmar

From ancient city-states to independence, explore the defining periods that shaped the culture, faith, and identity of Myanmar.

01
c. 200 BCE – 900 CE

Pyu City-States

The Pyu were among Southeast Asia's earliest urban civilisations, founding walled cities along the Irrawaddy River. Deeply Buddhist, their art and architecture laid the cultural foundations Myanmar would build upon for centuries.

Key Facts

UNESCO World Heritage cities Early Theravāda Buddhism Advanced irrigation systems Trade routes to India & China
02
849 – 1297 CE

Bagan Empire

The first unified Myanmar kingdom, Bagan is defined by its breathtaking plain of over 3,000 temples. King Anawrahta unified the country and established Theravāda Buddhism as the state religion, a legacy that endures today.

Key Facts

Over 3,000 temples built King Anawrahta's unification Burmese script developed Fell to Mongol invasions
03
1510 – 1752 CE

Toungoo Dynasty

The Toungoo Dynasty forged the largest empire in Southeast Asian history at its peak, uniting fragmented kingdoms under a powerful centralised rule. It represented a golden age of military strength and cultural flourishing.

Key Facts

Largest empire in SEA history First contact with Europeans Administrative reforms Rich literary tradition
04
1752 – 1885 CE

Konbaung Dynasty

The last royal dynasty of Myanmar, the Konbaung era saw both cultural brilliance and the painful reality of British colonial encroachment. Three Anglo-Burmese wars ultimately ended its reign, with Mandalay falling in 1885.

Key Facts

Last Myanmar royal dynasty Three Anglo-Burmese Wars Mandalay as royal capital Classical arts flourished
05
1824 – 1948 CE

British Colonial Period

Over more than a century, British rule reshaped Myanmar's economy, society, and borders. It also ignited a powerful nationalist movement, led by figures like General Aung San, that would ultimately secure independence in 1948.

Key Facts

Rangoon as colonial capital Nationalist movement rise General Aung San's leadership Independence: Jan 4, 1948
